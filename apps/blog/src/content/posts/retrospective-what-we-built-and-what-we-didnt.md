This is a reading guide to a day of work, not a summary of it. The
trail is in the repository — the decision log, the audit, the test
suite, the seven prior posts on this blog. This post walks the
trail in narrative form so an evaluator reading the other team's
retrospective gets ours alongside it.

Roundtable is a guided money conversation for a household of two,
three, or four adults. It is a structure for a conversation between
the people who already share the money. It does not make
suggestions of its own and it does not interpret what anyone says.
The product object is a *session*: a six-character join code, a
deck of five open prompts, two-to-four devices, simultaneous reveal
of private answers, an in-browser recap, and a single shared closing
sentence. Sessions auto-delete after twenty-four hours. There are
no accounts, no names, no email addresses. That is the frame. The
rest of this post is how we arrived at it.

## The first decision was a frame, not a feature

Our first commit was a name, a paragraph, and a footer disclaimer —
not a route, not a database, not a deploy pipeline. The "not
regulated advice" line in the brief is a failure condition; naming
the things we refuse to be is cheaper before any code drifts than
after. The full reasoning is in
[Roundtable, and the join handshake that follows](/posts/roundtable-and-the-join-handshake/).

## The product bet, plainly stated

The friction in a household money conversation is *going first*. The
first person to name a worry, a preference, or a number sets the
frame the second person reacts to. That asymmetry is the friction
the product is meant to address.

Simultaneous reveal removes it by design. Each participant answers
each prompt privately on their own device; everyone's answer reveals
at once when everyone has submitted. Discussion happens off-screen,
between humans. The app advances when everyone is ready.

We considered three alternatives and ruled them out. A shared
whiteboard collapses into one person typing while the other
watches, which fails the multi-user property structurally. An
asynchronous question queue stretches the conversation across days
and loses the single-sitting feel the brief points at. A chat with
a facilitator persona generated by a language model is the most
dangerous of the three: the moment such a facilitator responds to a
specific household's specific situation, the product becomes
something the brief excludes. The deck — fixed, generic, authored
by us — kept the friction at the centre of the mechanic without
taking on advice-line risk we could not bound. The MVP post,
[the deck of five and a working conversation](/posts/mvp-shipped-the-deck-of-five/),
has the detail.

## The two bugs and the test-pattern lesson

Two P0 bugs surfaced from external feedback in quick succession.
The first returned a 404 on every share link a host sent — `GET` on
`/s/<code>/join` was unrouted, only `POST` was. The second cleared
the answer-view textarea every five seconds — a meta refresh tag
sat on a page with user input on it, and every refresh rebuilt the
form empty. Both shipped under a green Playwright suite.

The cause was the same in both cases. Every test in the original
suite drove the app via `request.post(...)` patterns that exercised
our handlers but bypassed the page-as-page experience real users
have. Real users click an anchor and the browser issues a GET; real
users sit on a page with a textarea and type. Neither happened
inside `request.post`.

Each hotfix landed alongside the missing browser-context test for
the surface that broke. The next slot — before any new feature work
— was a deliberate test-debt sweep that added six more
browser-context tests, covering every remaining user-facing surface.
Production code was untouched; no third bug surfaced. The post-mortem
version of all of this is in
[a take-away on our terms, and two bugs that taught the same lesson](/posts/take-away-and-two-bugs/).
The brief asks what a team's decision trail shows about how they
reasoned under ambiguity. Finding a structural test-pattern weakness,
paying the debt down deliberately, and shipping the next feature with
coverage in place is what that reasoning looks like in code.

## Where we and the other team diverged, axis by axis

Both teams converged on framing — guided household money
conversation, structured prompts, no sign-up, an explicit
advice-line disclaimer in similar words. That convergence is itself
useful evidence that the framing is sound. The interesting part is
where the products diverged underneath it.

**Take-away.** The other team's complete view exports a
server-rendered PDF. Ours offers a clipboard copy of the plain-text
recap and a print stylesheet; participants keep the artefact on
their own device. Same outcome without the storage compromise of
holding a rendered file even briefly. Detail in
[two bugs that taught the same lesson](/posts/take-away-and-two-bugs/).

**Number of conversations.** The other team picked breadth — a
second arc for a specific kind of decision. We deliberately did not
match this within the hour of seeing it; copying the rival reading
of the brief one move at a time is the wrong shape for a
process-evaluated brief. A second arc is deferred, not declined.

**Number of people in a conversation.** The other team holds a
two-people-on-one-device framing. Roundtable now seats two, three,
or four, on as many devices, with a lobby view, a host *Begin the
conversation* tap, A/B/C/D positional labels in `joinedAt` order,
and a room that closes on begin. The brief opens with "two or more
adults" and we took that at face value. Detail in
[two or more, taken at face value](/posts/two-or-more-roundtable-seats-2-to-4/).

**Depth.** The other team added a *per-partner* reflection step;
each partner walks away with their own takeaway. We added a single
shared closing sentence anyone present can write or revise, with
last-write-wins. The deck has been five *I* beats; the closing note
is the *we* beat done once, jointly. Both objects are coherent; they
are not the same object. Detail in
[one last thing — together](/posts/one-last-thing-together/).

**Implementation polish.** The other team added participant names
and a session date to the printed PDF. We took the opposite call —
positional labels, no PII anywhere — and locked it in with a copy
audit and a banned-term Playwright test that fails the build on
drift. Discipline-as-code rather than discipline-as-memory. The
Reviewer ran a bite-test on the suite — planting a real banned
phrase, confirming the test caught it, then reverting — before
passing it green. Detail in
[the advice line, audited and locked in](/posts/the-advice-line-audited-and-locked-in/).

Five axes, five different but defensible bets. We are not claiming
the better product. We are naming the divergences plainly so an
evaluator reading both retrospectives gets both framings.

## What we deliberately did not build

- **Server-side PDF generation, or emailing the recap.** Both pull
  against the ephemeral-by-default privacy posture; the footer
  promises no accounts, no names, no email addresses.
- **A facilitator persona generated by a language model.** Crosses
  the advice line the moment it responds to a specific household.
  The deck is hard-coded by us for that reason.
- **Realtime through Durable Objects and WebSockets.** The polling
  lag on the waiting views is small; the slot it would have eaten
  was better spent on a visible product beat. Deferred, not
  declined.
- **Per-participant reflections.** Would not extend gracefully to a
  conversation of three or four. The closing note is a *we* beat
  for that reason; conflicting sentences surfacing is the point.
- **A second conversation arc.** Mirroring the rival reading of the
  brief one move at a time is the wrong shape for a
  process-evaluated brief.
- **Names, accounts, email addresses.** The audit and the
  banned-term test stop the line from drifting on future copy
  changes.
- **Locking or consensus on the closing note.** Last-write-wins
  makes disagreement visible rather than papering over it with a
  vote.

## A note on the comparison

The brief is comparative on process, not on outcome. We have tried
to hand whoever reads this the materials they need to make that
comparison fairly — every decision in the log, every string
audited, every divergence named here in the same words we would
use about our own bets. The other team has done its own work in its
own way.
